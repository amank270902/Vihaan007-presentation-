Arunachal Pradesh

  • Tawang Monastery: This is the largest monastery in India and the second largest in the world after the Potala Palace in Lhasa, Tibet. It’s a significant site for Mahayana Buddhism and offers breathtaking views of the Tawang River valley
  • Sela Pass: Known as the gateway to Tawang, Sela Pass is one of the high-altitude mountain passes in Arunachal Pradesh. It’s famous for its scenic beauty and the nearby Sela Lake
  • Namdapha National Park: It’s the third-largest national park in India and a biodiversity hotspot, home to a wide variety of flora and fauna, including the elusive red panda
  • Ziro Valley: A UNESCO World Heritage Site nominee, Ziro Valley is famous for its lush landscapes, unique Apatani tribal culture, and the annual Ziro Music Festival
  • Parshuram Kund: A sacred place for Hindus, located on the Brahmaputra plateau, it’s known for its beautiful natural surroundings and religious significance
  • Cultural Diversity: Arunachal Pradesh is home to 26 major tribes and over 100 sub-tribes, each with its own distinct traditions, languages, and crafts. The state’s culture is a vibrant tapestry of various tribal customs and practices
  • Traditional Cuisine: The food of Arunachal Pradesh is influenced by the tribal way of life and features rice, meat, and leafy vegetables. Local specialties include bamboo shoot, smoked meat, and Apong, a traditional rice beer

    • Assam

  • Breathtaking Landscapes: Assam boasts lush tea gardens, rolling hills of the Brahmaputra Valley, and the mesmerizing Kaziranga National Park, home to the one-horned rhinoceros.
  • Rich Cultural Heritage: The state is renowned for its vibrant culture, including the Bihu festival, which celebrates the Assamese New Year, and traditional dance forms like the Sattriya and Bihu dance.
  • Tea Tourism: Assam is the largest tea-producing region in India, offering tourists the opportunity to visit tea estates, learn about tea cultivation, and indulge in tea tasting sessions.
  • Wildlife Sanctuaries: Besides Kaziranga, Assam is home to several wildlife sanctuaries and national parks like Manas and Nameri, where visitors can spot diverse flora and fauna, including the majestic Bengal tiger and elephants.
  • Cultural Diversity: Assam's cultural tapestry is enriched by its diverse communities, including the Assamese, Bodos, Ahoms, and various tribal groups, each contributing unique traditions, cuisines, and crafts.
  • River Cruises: The mighty Brahmaputra River offers scenic river cruises, allowing tourists to explore Assam's picturesque landscapes, spot wildlife along the banks, and experience the local way of life in riverine villages.
  • Historical Sites: Assam is dotted with historical landmarks such as the Kamakhya Temple in Guwahati, the Ahom-era monuments in Sivasagar, and the ancient capital of Tezpur, offering glimpses into the region's rich past.

    • Meghalaya

  • Living Root Bridges: Meghalaya is famous for its unique living root bridges, formed by intertwining the aerial roots of rubber trees, attracting tourists interested in eco-tourism and nature photography.
  • Cherrapunji and Mawsynram: Known as the wettest places on Earth, these locations in Meghalaya offer breathtaking landscapes, including cascading waterfalls, lush green valleys, and caves waiting to be explored.
  • Indigenous Khasi Culture: The Khasi people, one of the major ethnic groups in Meghalaya, have a rich cultural heritage showcased in their traditional music, dance forms like Wangala, and unique customs like the matrilineal system.
  • Sacred Groves: Meghalaya is home to numerous sacred groves, revered by indigenous communities for their spiritual significance and biodiversity, providing opportunities for nature enthusiasts and cultural tourists alike.
  • Adventure Tourism: The state offers thrilling adventure activities such as trekking through the picturesque hills, exploring limestone caves like the Mawsmai Cave, and experiencing river rafting in the crystal-clear waters of the Umngot River in Dawki.
  • Living with Nature: Meghalaya's villages and towns are nestled amidst serene natural surroundings, providing visitors with opportunities to experience sustainable living practices and immerse themselves in the tranquility of rural life.
  • Meghalayan Cuisine: Food enthusiasts can savor the unique flavors of Meghalayan cuisine, known for dishes like Jadoh (rice cooked with meat), Doh Neiiong (pork cooked with black sesame seeds), and various preparations of local ingredients like bamboo shoots and fermented soybeans.

    • Manipur

  • Loktak Lake: Manipur's iconic Loktak Lake, known for its floating phumdis (islands), offers scenic boat rides, birdwatching, and a glimpse into the traditional lifestyle of the fishermen communities living on the lake.
  • Sangai Deer Sanctuary: Home to the endangered Sangai deer, the sanctuary in Manipur's Keibul Lamjao National Park is a must-visit for wildlife enthusiasts, providing opportunities for wildlife safaris and birdwatching.
  • Ras Lila: Manipur is famous for its classical dance form, Ras Lila, which depicts the love story of Lord Krishna and Radha. Visitors can witness mesmerizing performances showcasing the state's rich cultural heritage.
  • Handloom and Handicrafts: Manipur's intricate handloom textiles and handicrafts, including Phanek (traditional wrap-around skirt) and Moirang Phee (shawl), attract tourists interested in exploring the state's vibrant craft traditions.
  • Ima Keithel (Mother's Market): Asia's largest all-women market, Ima Keithel in Imphal, offers a fascinating shopping experience, where visitors can purchase a wide array of local products, including traditional attire, handicrafts, and fresh produce.
  • Manipur Polo: Known as the "Sport of Kings," Manipur is considered the birthplace of modern polo. Tourists can witness thrilling polo matches played in traditional style on Manipur's vast grasslands, providing a unique sporting and cultural experience.
  • Historical Sites: Manipur boasts historical sites such as Kangla Fort, the ancient seat of Manipuri rulers, and the War Cemetery in Imphal, commemorating the sacrifices of soldiers during World War II, offering insights into the state's rich history and heritage.

    • Mizoram

  • Dzukou Valley: Mizoram's Dzukou Valley is a trekker's paradise, with its picturesque landscapes, lush green hills, and colorful blooms during the spring, attracting nature lovers and adventure seekers.
  • Champhai: Known as the "Rice Bowl of Mizoram," Champhai offers stunning vistas of terraced rice fields, traditional Mizo villages, and the enchanting Murlen National Park, providing visitors with a glimpse of rural life and biodiversity.
  • Mizo Culture: Mizoram's vibrant Mizo culture is reflected in its traditional festivals like Chapchar Kut and Mim Kut, showcasing colorful dances, folk music, and rituals that celebrate the community's agricultural heritage and religious beliefs.
  • Tribal Handicrafts: Visitors can explore Mizoram's rich artistic traditions through its intricate bamboo and cane crafts, traditional handwoven textiles like Puans and Tawis, and vibrant tribal jewelry, offering unique souvenirs and cultural insights.
  • Blue Mountain: The "Blue Mountain" of Mizoram, Phawngpui Peak, is the highest peak in the state and offers panoramic views of the surrounding hills and valleys, along with opportunities for trekking and birdwatching in its pristine forests.
  • Palak Lake: Also known as Pala Tipo, Palak Lake is a serene natural reservoir surrounded by lush forests and bamboo groves, offering boating and fishing experiences amidst tranquil surroundings.
  • Mizoram State Museum: Located in Aizawl, the Mizoram State Museum provides visitors with a comprehensive insight into the state's history, culture, and traditions through its collection of artifacts, photographs, and exhibits.

    • Nagaland

  • Hornbill Festival: Nagaland's Hornbill Festival, held in December, is a vibrant celebration of Naga culture, featuring traditional dances, indigenous music, tribal cuisine, and colorful crafts, drawing tourists from around the world.
  • Naga Heritage Village: Kohima's Naga Heritage Village is a living museum showcasing the diverse tribal traditions of Nagaland, with replica tribal huts, artifacts, and demonstrations of traditional practices, offering insights into Naga heritage and lifestyle.
  • Dzukou Valley: Nagaland shares the picturesque Dzukou Valley with Manipur, offering trekkers and nature enthusiasts breathtaking landscapes, seasonal blooms, and camping opportunities amidst pristine natural beauty.
  • War Cemetery: The War Cemetery in Kohima commemorates the Battle of Kohima during World War II, with beautifully maintained graves and memorials honoring the soldiers who fought and died in one of the fiercest battles of the war, attracting history buffs and veterans.
  • Tribal Villages: Nagaland's rural villages provide authentic cultural experiences, where visitors can interact with Naga tribes like the Angamis, Aos, and Lothas, participate in traditional ceremonies, and witness indigenous craftsmanship like Naga shawl weaving.
  • Folk Music and Dance: Nagaland's rich musical heritage is showcased through traditional Naga folk songs, accompanied by indigenous instruments like the log drum and bamboo flute, while tribal dances like the warrior dance and bamboo dance captivate audiences with their rhythmic movements.
  • Khonoma Green Village: Khonoma Village is known for its community-based eco-tourism initiatives,responsible tourism, offering visitors opportunities for trekking, birdwatching, and cultural exchanges with the local community.

    • Tripura

  • Neermahal: Neermahal, the "Water Palace," is a stunning architectural marvel located in the middle of Rudrasagar Lake, offering visitors a glimpse of Tripura's royal heritage and picturesque surroundings ideal for boating and photography.
  • Unakoti: Unakoti is an ancient archaeological site adorned with thousands of rock carvings and sculptures dating back to the 7th-9th centuries, depicting Hindu deities and mythological figures, making it a significant pilgrimage and heritage site.
  • Sepahijala Wildlife Sanctuary: Tripura's Sepahijala Wildlife Sanctuary is home to diverse flora and fauna, including rare species like the clouded leopard and spectacled langur, providing opportunities for wildlife safaris, birdwatching, and nature trails.
  • Tripuri Culture: The rich cultural heritage of Tripura is showcased through traditional dance forms like Hojagiri and Garia Puja, along with colorful festivals like Kharchi Puja and Ker Puja, offering visitors a chance to experience the state's vibrant customs and rituals.
  • Ujjayanta Palace: Ujjayanta Palace, located in Agartala, serves as a museum showcasing Tripura's history, art, and culture through its impressive collections of royal artifacts, sculptures, and manuscripts, providing insights into the region's past.
  • Jampui Hills: Jampui Hills, located in North Tripura, is renowned for its scenic beauty, pleasant climate, and vibrant orange orchards, making it a popular destination for trekking, camping, and enjoying panoramic views of the surrounding valleys.
